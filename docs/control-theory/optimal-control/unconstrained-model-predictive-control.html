Unconstrained Model Predictive Control

Unconstrained Model Predictive Control (MPC) provides a foundation for understanding the core principles of predictive control without the complexity of constraint handling. This document presents the complete mathematical derivation and implementation of unconstrained MPC for linear systems.

Problem Formulation​

System Model​

Consider a discrete-time linear time-invariant system:

xk+1=Axk+Bukx_{k+1} = Ax_k + Bu_kxk+1​=Axk​+Buk​

Where:

  • xk∈Rnx_k \in \mathbb{R}^nxk​∈Rn: state vector at time step kkk
  • uk∈Rpu_k \in \mathbb{R}^puk​∈Rp: control input vector at time step kkk
  • A∈Rn×nA \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}A∈Rn×n: state transition matrix
  • B∈Rn×pB \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}B∈Rn×p: input matrix

Performance Function​

The finite-horizon cost function is:

Jk=12xk+hTQfxk+h+12∑i=0h−1(xk+iTQxk+i+uk+iTRuk+i)J_k = \frac{1}{2}x_{k+h}^T Q_f x_{k+h} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=0}^{h-1} \left(x_{k+i}^T Q x_{k+i} + u_{k+i}^T R u_{k+i}\right)Jk​=21​xk+hT​Qf​xk+h​+21​i=0∑h−1​(xk+iT​Qxk+i​+uk+iT​Ruk+i​)

Where:

  • hhh: prediction horizon (number of future steps to consider)
  • Qf∈Rn×nQ_f \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}Qf​∈Rn×n: terminal weight matrix (Qf≥0Q_f \geq 0Qf​≥0)
  • Q∈Rn×nQ \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}Q∈Rn×n: state weight matrix (Q≥0Q \geq 0Q≥0)
  • R∈Rp×pR \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}R∈Rp×p: input weight matrix (R>0R > 0R>0)
Weight Matrix Properties
  • Qf,QQ_f, QQf​,Q: Positive semi-definite (allow regulation to zero)
  • RRR: Positive definite (ensures unique solution)
  • These matrices determine the relative importance of different objectives

Prediction Horizon Formulation​

State Predictions​

At time step kkk, the future states are predicted as:

xk+1∣k=Axk∣k+Buk∣kxk+2∣k=Axk+1∣k+Buk+1∣k⋮xk+h∣k=Axk+h−1∣k+Buk+h−1∣k\begin{aligned} x_{k+1|k} &= Ax_{k|k} + Bu_{k|k} \\ x_{k+2|k} &= Ax_{k+1|k} + Bu_{k+1|k} \\ &\vdots \\ x_{k+h|k} &= Ax_{k+h-1|k} + Bu_{k+h-1|k} \end{aligned}xk+1∣k​xk+2∣k​xk+h∣k​​=Axk∣k​+Buk∣k​=Axk+1∣k​+Buk+1∣k​⋮=Axk+h−1∣k​+Buk+h−1∣k​​

Compact Matrix Notation​

For efficient computation, define the stacked vectors:

Predicted state vector:

Xk:=[xk+1∣kxk+2∣k⋮xk+h∣k]∈RnhX_k := \begin{bmatrix} x_{k+1|k} \\ x_{k+2|k} \\ \vdots \\ x_{k+h|k} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{nh}Xk​:=​xk+1∣k​xk+2∣k​⋮xk+h∣k​​​∈Rnh

Control input vector:

Uk:=[uk∣kuk+1∣k⋮uk+h−1∣k]∈RphU_k := \begin{bmatrix} u_{k|k} \\ u_{k+1|k} \\ \vdots \\ u_{k+h-1|k} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{ph}Uk​:=​uk∣k​uk+1∣k​⋮uk+h−1∣k​​​∈Rph

Matrix Form of Predictions​

The prediction equation becomes:

Xk=Apxk∣k+BpUkX_k = A_p x_{k|k} + B_p U_kXk​=Ap​xk∣k​+Bp​Uk​

Where:

Prediction matrix for states:

Ap=[AA2⋮Ah]∈Rnh×nA_p = \begin{bmatrix} A \\ A^2 \\ \vdots \\ A^h \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{nh \times n}Ap​=​AA2⋮Ah​​∈Rnh×n

Prediction matrix for inputs:

Bp=[B0n×p⋯0n×pABB⋯0n×p⋮⋮⋱⋮Ah−1BAh−2B⋯B]∈Rnh×ph\begin{aligned}B_p = \begin{bmatrix}B & 0 _ { n \times p} & \cdots & 0_{n \times p} \\AB & B & \cdots & 0 _ { n \times p} \\\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\A^{h-1}B & A^{h-2}B & \cdots & B\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{nh \times ph}\end{aligned}Bp​=​BAB⋮Ah−1B​0n×p​B⋮Ah−2B​⋯⋯⋱⋯​0n×p​0n×p​⋮B​​∈Rnh×ph​
Matrix Interpretation
  • Each row block of ApA_pAp​ contains AiA^iAi representing the effect of initial state after iii steps
  • Each element of BpB_pBp​ contains Ai−jBA^{i-j}BAi−jB representing the effect of input uk+j−1u_{k+j-1}uk+j−1​ on state xk+ix_{k+i}xk+i​

Quadratic Programming Transformation​

Objective: Standard QP Form​

To solve the MPC problem efficiently, we transform it into standard quadratic programming form:

min⁡Uk12UkTHUk+FTxk∣kUk+constant\min_{U_k} \frac{1}{2}U_k^T H U_k + F^T x_{k|k} U_k + \text{constant}Uk​min​21​UkT​HUk​+FTxk∣k​Uk​+constant

Step 1: Separate Initial State Cost​

Rewrite the cost function by extracting the initial state term:

Jk=12xk∣kTQxk∣k+12xk+h∣kTQfxk+h∣k+12∑i=1h−1xk+i∣kTQxk+i∣k+12∑i=0h−1uk+i∣kTRuk+i∣kJ_k = \frac{1}{2}x_{k|k}^T Q x_{k|k} + \frac{1}{2}x_{k+h|k}^T Q_f x_{k+h|k} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{h-1} x_{k+i|k}^T Q x_{k+i|k} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=0}^{h-1} u_{k+i|k}^T R u_{k+i|k}Jk​=21​xk∣kT​Qxk∣k​+21​xk+h∣kT​Qf​xk+h∣k​+21​i=1∑h−1​xk+i∣kT​Qxk+i∣k​+21​i=0∑h−1​uk+i∣kT​Ruk+i∣k​

Step 2: Define Block Diagonal Matrices​

State weight matrix for prediction horizon:

Qp:=[Q⋯0n×n⋮Q⋮0n×n⋯Qf]∈Rnh×nhQ_p := \begin{bmatrix} Q & \cdots & 0_{n \times n} \\ \vdots & Q & \vdots \\ 0_{n \times n} & \cdots & Q_f \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{nh \times nh}Qp​:=​Q⋮0n×n​​⋯Q⋯​0n×n​⋮Qf​​​∈Rnh×nh

Input weight matrix for prediction horizon:

Rp:=[R⋯0p×p⋮R⋮0p×p⋯R]∈Rph×phR_p := \begin{bmatrix} R & \cdots & 0_{p \times p} \\ \vdots & R & \vdots \\ 0_{p \times p} & \cdots & R \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{ph \times ph}Rp​:=​R⋮0p×p​​⋯R⋯​0p×p​⋮R​​∈Rph×ph

Step 3: Express Cost in Matrix Form​

The cost function becomes:

Jk=12xk∣kTQxk∣k+12XkTQpXk+12UkTRpUkJ_k = \frac{1}{2}x_{k|k}^T Q x_{k|k} + \frac{1}{2}X_k^T Q_p X_k + \frac{1}{2}U_k^T R_p U_kJk​=21​xk∣kT​Qxk∣k​+21​XkT​Qp​Xk​+21​UkT​Rp​Uk​

Step 4: Substitute Prediction Equation​

Substituting Xk=Apxk∣k+BpUkX_k = A_p x_{k|k} + B_p U_kXk​=Ap​xk∣k​+Bp​Uk​:

XkTQpXk=(Apxk∣k+BpUk)TQp(Apxk∣k+BpUk)X_k^T Q_p X_k = (A_p x_{k|k} + B_p U_k)^T Q_p (A_p x_{k|k} + B_p U_k)XkT​Qp​Xk​=(Ap​xk∣k​+Bp​Uk​)TQp​(Ap​xk∣k​+Bp​Uk​)

Expanding:

XkTQpXk=xk∣kTApTQpApxk∣k+2xk∣kTApTQpBpUk+UkTBpTQpBpUk\begin{aligned} X_k^T Q_p X_k &= x_{k|k}^T A_p^T Q_p A_p x_{k|k} \\ &\quad + 2x_{k|k}^T A_p^T Q_p B_p U_k \\ &\quad + U_k^T B_p^T Q_p B_p U_k \end{aligned}XkT​Qp​Xk​​=xk∣kT​ApT​Qp​Ap​xk∣k​+2xk∣kT​ApT​Qp​Bp​Uk​+UkT​BpT​Qp​Bp​Uk​​

Step 5: Final QP Form​

Collecting terms:

Jk=12UkTHUk+FTxk∣kUk+CJ_k = \frac{1}{2}U_k^T H U_k + F^T x_{k|k} U_k + CJk​=21​UkT​HUk​+FTxk∣k​Uk​+C

Where:

Hessian matrix:

H:=Rp+BpTQpBp∈Rph×phH := R_p + B_p^T Q_p B_p \in \mathbb{R}^{ph \times ph}H:=Rp​+BpT​Qp​Bp​∈Rph×ph

Linear term coefficient:

F:=BpTQpAp∈Rph×nF := B_p^T Q_p A_p \in \mathbb{R}^{ph \times n}F:=BpT​Qp​Ap​∈Rph×n

Constant term:

C:=12xk∣kT(Q+ApTQpAp)xk∣kC := \frac{1}{2}x_{k|k}^T (Q + A_p^T Q_p A_p) x_{k|k}C:=21​xk∣kT​(Q+ApT​Qp​Ap​)xk∣k​
Key Point

The constant term CCC depends only on the current state xk∣kx_{k|k}xk∣k​ and doesn't affect the optimization. We can ignore it when solving for the optimal control sequence.

Analytical Solution​

Unconstrained Optimization​

For the unconstrained case, the optimal control sequence is found by setting the gradient to zero:

∂Jk∂Uk=HUk+Fxk∣k=0\frac{\partial J_k}{\partial U_k} = H U_k + F x_{k|k} = 0∂Uk​∂Jk​​=HUk​+Fxk∣k​=0

Optimal control sequence:

Uk∗=−H−1Fxk∣kU_k^* = -H^{-1} F x_{k|k}Uk∗​=−H−1Fxk∣k​

Extracting the First Control​

Since MPC applies only the first control action:

uk∣k∗=[Ip×p0p×p⋯0p×p]Uk∗u_{k|k}^* = \begin{bmatrix} I_{p \times p} & 0_{p \times p} & \cdots & 0_{p \times p} \end{bmatrix} U_k^*uk∣k∗​=[Ip×p​​0p×p​​⋯​0p×p​​]Uk∗​

This gives us the MPC feedback law:

uk∣k∗=−Kmpcxk∣ku_{k|k}^* = -K_{mpc} x_{k|k}uk∣k∗​=−Kmpc​xk∣k​

Where the MPC feedback gain is:

Kmpc:=[Ip×p0p×p⋯0p×p]H−1FK_{mpc} := \begin{bmatrix} I_{p \times p} & 0_{p \times p} & \cdots & 0_{p \times p} \end{bmatrix} H^{-1} FKmpc​:=[Ip×p​​0p×p​​⋯​0p×p​​]H−1F

Implementation​

MATLAB Implementation​

QP Transformation Function​

function [Ap, Bp, Qp, Rp, F, H] = QP_Transform(A, B, Q, R, Qf, Np)
%% QP_Transform - Transform MPC cost function into standard QP form
% 
% This function transforms the MPC cost function into standard QP form
% for efficient numerical solution.
%
% Inputs:
%   A, B - System matrices
%   Q, R, Qf - Weight matrices  
%   Np - Prediction horizon
%
% Outputs:
%   Ap, Bp - Prediction matrices
%   Qp, Rp - Block diagonal weight matrices
%   F, H - QP problem matrices

n = size(A, 1);  % Number of states
p = size(B, 2);  % Number of inputs

% Initialize prediction matrices
Ap = zeros(Np*n, n);
Bp = zeros(Np*n, Np*p);

% Build prediction matrix Ap = [A; A^2; ...; A^Np]
for i = 1:Np
    Ap((i-1)*n+1:i*n, :) = A^i;
end

% Build prediction matrix Bp
for i = 1:Np
    for j = 1:i
        Bp((i-1)*n+1:i*n, (j-1)*p+1:j*p) = A^(i-j) * B;
    end
end

% Build block diagonal weight matrices
% Qp = diag(Q, Q, ..., Q, Qf)
Qp = kron(eye(Np-1), Q);
Qp = blkdiag(Qp, Qf);

% Rp = diag(R, R, ..., R)
Rp = kron(eye(Np), R);

% Compute QP matrices
F = Bp' * Qp * Ap;       % Linear term coefficient
H = Bp' * Qp * Bp + Rp;  % Hessian matrix

end

Python Implementation​

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.optimize import minimize
from scipy.linalg import solve_discrete_are

def qp_transform(A, B, Q, R, Qf, Np):
    """Transform MPC cost function into standard QP form"""
    n, p = A.shape[0], B.shape[1]
    
    # Build prediction matrix Ap
    Ap = np.zeros((Np*n, n))
    A_power = np.eye(n)
    for i in range(Np):
        A_power = A_power @ A
        Ap[i*n:(i+1)*n, :] = A_power
    
    # Build prediction matrix Bp
    Bp = np.zeros((Np*n, Np*p))
    for i in range(Np):
        for j in range(i+1):
            Bp[i*n:(i+1)*n, j*p:(j+1)*p] = np.linalg.matrix_power(A, i-j) @ B
    
    # Build block diagonal weight matrices
    Qp = np.kron(np.eye(Np-1), Q)
    Qp = np.block([[Qp, np.zeros((Qp.shape[0], Qf.shape[1]))],
                   [np.zeros((Qf.shape[0], Qp.shape[1])), Qf]])
    
    Rp = np.kron(np.eye(Np), R)
    
    # Compute QP matrices
    F = Bp.T @ Qp @ Ap
    H = Bp.T @ Qp @ Bp + Rp
    
    return Ap, Bp, Qp, Rp, F, H

def mpc_unconstrained(A, B, Q, R, Qf, x0, k_steps, Np):
    """Unconstrained MPC simulation"""
    n, p = A.shape[0], B.shape[1]
    
    # Get QP matrices
    _, _, _, _, F, H = qp_transform(A, B, Q, R, Qf, Np)
    
    # Initialize arrays
    x_traj = np.zeros((n, k_steps+1))
    u_traj = np.zeros((p, k_steps))
    x_traj[:, 0] = x0
    
    x = x0.copy()
    
    for k in range(k_steps):
        # Solve QP problem: min 0.5*U'*H*U + (F*x)'*U
        def objective(U):
            return 0.5 * U.T @ H @ U + (F @ x).T @ U
        
        # Initial guess
        U0 = np.zeros(Np * p)
        
        # Solve optimization
        result = minimize(objective, U0, method='BFGS')
        U_opt = result.x
        
        # Extract first control action
        u = U_opt[:p]
        u_traj[:, k] = u
        
        # Update state
        x = A @ x + B @ u
        x_traj[:, k+1] = x
    
    return x_traj, u_traj

# System matrices
A = np.array([[0, 1], [0.5, 0]])
B = np.array([[0], [1]])

# Weight matrices
Q = np.eye(2)
Qf = np.eye(2)
R = np.array([[1]])

# Simulation parameters
x0 = np.array([10, 5])
k_steps = 30
Np = 5

# MPC simulation
x_mpc, u_mpc = mpc_unconstrained(A, B, Q, R, Qf, x0, k_steps, Np)

# LQR for comparison
P = solve_discrete_are(A, B, Q, R)
K_lqr = np.linalg.solve(R + B.T @ P @ B, B.T @ P @ A)

# LQR simulation
x_lqr = np.zeros((2, k_steps+1))
u_lqr = np.zeros((1, k_steps))
x_lqr[:, 0] = x0

x = x0.copy()
for k in range(k_steps):
    u = -K_lqr @ x
    u_lqr[:, k] = u
    x = A @ x + B @ u
    x_lqr[:, k+1] = x

# Plotting
fig, (ax1, ax2, ax3) = plt.subplots(3, 1, figsize=(10, 8))

# State x1
ax1.plot(range(k_steps+1), x_lqr[0, :], 'b-', linewidth=2, label='LQR')

Analysis: MPC vs LQR​

Performance Comparison​

The simulation results demonstrate several key differences between unconstrained MPC and LQR:

Weight Matrix Effects​

Case 1: Q=Qf=[100001],R=1Q = Q_f = \begin{bmatrix} 100 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, R = 1Q=Qf​=[1000​01​],R=1

  • High penalty on x1x_1x1​: Both controllers prioritize position regulation
  • MPC shows similar performance to LQR but with slightly different transients
  • Both achieve fast convergence of position state

Case 2: Q=Qf=[1001],R=1Q = Q_f = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, R = 1Q=Qf​=[10​01​],R=1

  • Balanced state penalties: Equal importance on both states
  • MPC exhibits more conservative behavior initially
  • Similar steady-state performance for both controllers

Case 3: Q=Qf=[1001],R=100Q = Q_f = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, R = 100Q=Qf​=[10​01​],R=100

  • High input penalty: Both controllers use minimal control effort
  • Slower convergence but smoother control action
  • MPC shows slightly different input profile due to finite horizon

Key Observations​

  1. Finite Horizon Effects:

    • MPC considers only hhh future steps, while LQR assumes infinite horizon
    • This can lead to slightly different control strategies, especially early in the response

  2. Terminal Cost Importance:

    • The choice of QfQ_fQf​ affects MPC performance significantly
    • Setting QfQ_fQf​ equal to the infinite-horizon LQR cost matrix often improves performance

  3. Computational Differences:

    • LQR: One-time gain computation, constant feedback law
    • MPC: Online optimization at each time step, adaptive behavior

  4. Equivalence Conditions:

    • For very long prediction horizons, unconstrained MPC approaches LQR performance
    • With proper terminal cost design, even short horizons can achieve good performance

Design Guidelines​

Prediction Horizon Selection​

Short horizons (h=2−5h = 2-5h=2−5):

  • Fast computation
  • May sacrifice performance
  • Suitable for fast systems or limited computational resources

Medium horizons (h=10−20h = 10-20h=10−20):

  • Good balance between performance and computation
  • Covers most system dynamics
  • Recommended for most applications

Long horizons (h>20h > 20h>20):

  • Approaches infinite-horizon performance
  • Higher computational cost
  • May be unnecessary for many systems

Weight Matrix Tuning​

State weights (Q,QfQ, Q_fQ,Qf​):

  • Reflect relative importance of different states
  • QfQ_fQf​ should account for future behavior beyond horizon
  • Consider setting QfQ_fQf​ as solution to infinite-horizon ARE

Input weights (RRR):

  • Balance performance vs. control effort
  • Larger values → more conservative control
  • Consider actuator limitations and energy constraints

Computational Considerations​

Matrix conditioning:

  • Ensure HHH is well-conditioned for numerical stability
  • Check eigenvalues of HHH for positive definiteness
  • Consider regularization if necessary

Efficient implementation:

  • Pre-compute constant matrices (ApA_pAp​, BpB_pBp​) when possible
  • Use warm-starting for iterative solvers
  • Exploit structure in QP problem for faster solution

Summary​

Unconstrained MPC provides:

  • Systematic framework for finite-horizon optimal control
  • Natural extension of LQR to finite horizons
  • Foundation for understanding constrained MPC
  • Computational efficiency through QP formulation

The analytical solution reveals the direct relationship between MPC and LQR, while the finite horizon introduces important design considerations for practical implementation.
